Alan Turing

The Father of Modern Computer Science

“Sometimes it is the people who no one imagines anything of who do the things that no one can imagine.”
― Alan Turing

Life

Alan Mathison Turing was an English mathematician who is widely considered today to be the father of both theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence. He was born on June 23, 1912 in London and raised in southern England.

After graduating from King's College in Cambridge with Bachelor of Arts and Master of Arts degrees in mathematics, Turing earned his PhD from Princeton University in the mathematics department.

During World War II, Turing worked at Bletchley Park, the British Government Code and Cypher School's wartime station, on a team of cryptologists with a mission of decripting German ciphers. Here, he created the bombe, an electromechanical computing device, with which his team was able to decipher German Enigma encrypted signals. After the war, Turing designed the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE), which was one of the first deisgns for a stored-program computer. These are just a couple examples of Turing's many contributions to the field of computer science and artificial intelligence.

For a brief time, Turing was engaged to Joan Clarke, a close friend and colleague whom he met while they worked together at Bletchley Park. However, the two ultimately never married.

Turing was gay in a time and place wherein homosexuality was illegal. In 1952, he was prosecuted for having engaged in homosexual acts. He was given a choice of hormone therapy (also known as chemical castration) or prison, and he chose the former so that he could continue his work. The multitude of negative effects on his wellbeing caused by the hormone therapy ultimately led to his suicide. He died on June 7, 1954, at just 41 years old.

Legacy

Turing's honor and recognition mostly came after his death, because much of his work was kept secret by the British government under the Official Secrets Act. However, after his death, his incredible contributions came to light.

Since 1966, the Association for Computing Machinery has awarded the Turing Award (commonly referred to as the Nobel Prize of computing), which is considered the highest distinction in computer science, and is awarded for significant contributions in the field of computer science.

In 2013, Turing received a posthumous royal pardon from the late Queen Elizabeth II. In 2016, the British government introduced "Turing's Law", which automatically pardoned thousands of homosexual men, both living and deceased, who had been convicted of crimes based on their homosexuality alone (who otherwise would be innocent of any crime) during the time that it had been criminalized.

Many universities across the globe have named buildings, programs, and laboratories in Turing's honor. Several statues of Turing exist to this day, including one at Bletchley Park.

In 2019, the Bank of England announced that Turing himself, as well as images of his work, would appear on the United Kingdom's new £50 note.

In more ways than can be listed here, Turing has been honored not only for his groundbreaking and vast contributions to the field of computer science, but also for the impact his life and death have had on the LGBTQ+ community.
